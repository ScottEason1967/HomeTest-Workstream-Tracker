17 JulRDUH urology team demonstrated the current Epic PSA-monitoring workflow; user needs written up

The RDUH urology team (Professor Tim McDonald, with urology nurses Paula and Kerry Rosewarne) demonstrated how PSA monitoring is run today inside Epic. Nurses pull a monthly report of PSA results, filter to their cohort and review each patient in turn, deciding whether action is needed, chasing investigations such as MRI, ordering the next PSA (typically at three months) and routing patients through community collection. The pathway is result-triggered rather than appointment-triggered: the PSA result is what keeps it moving, so a delayed or lost result stalls it. The team was positive about HomeTest off the back of their earlier PSA pilot, where patients liked the service and some were disappointed when it ended, and saw younger and working-age patients as the most likely adopters. Their primary concern was not patient acceptance but that results return reliably into Epic and existing clinical workflows; a separate monitoring platform would add work and would not be acceptable. Recorded constraints include multi-step follow-up ordering within Epic, community-collection coordination, filtering regional cohorts, travel burden for testing, and some patients struggling with self-sampling on dexterity grounds. This evidence underpins the Epic-to-HomeTest-to-Epic proof of concept and the result-return requirement in the PSA pilot design.

Source: Pain Points and User Needs.docx (RDHU interview summary), 16. Pathway 2 Discovery Alpha / RDHU evidence, 17 July 2026.

15 JulPSA Best Practice Tariff benefits paper scoped with the health economist

Rachel Flower updated the team after a call with Dr Simon Leigh, the health economist, on the PSA Best Practice Tariff case and the numbers behind it. The paper will argue that home testing helps trusts deliver the GIRFT active-surveillance pathway more efficiently, releasing capacity, improving RTT performance and unlocking the associated incentive payments. It is structured in four parts: the problem with today's outpatient-heavy, consultant-intensive pathway; what changes when testing moves closer to the patient, meaning which appointments, blood-taking visits, administrative touchpoints, DNAs and delays disappear and what becomes faster in risk identification, escalation and clinical decision-making; the capacity released, such as consultant sessions freed, outpatient appointments avoided, additional first appointments and other RTT drivers; and why the pathway cannot achieve the same benefits without patient-centric testing. Simon will also work up a break-even point, showing financial benefit accruing even if only a limited number of patients take part, rather than only in a best case. Richard Samuel is finding out whether GIRFT holds usable data as a starting point and confirming the submission date, and a live question is how much of the capacity and change data is factual against assumed or estimated. A BPT working folder was set up under the Health Economist commercials area the same day. This is the acute pilot's benefits and investment case, distinct from the wider HomeTest tariff and unit-economics work.

Source: R Flower team update on the PSA BPT benefits paper, 15 July 2026.

14-15 JulAcute-initiated discovery synthesis packaged for the 15 July SLT playback

Ahead of the 15 July playback to Shirley Gaynor-Ward that closes the acute-initiated discovery period, the work was consolidated into a PSA home testing pre-discovery report and a refreshed acute-initiated discovery checkpoint. The synthesis draws on research with 31 clinicians and hospital staff across urology, pre-operative assessment and sexual health, and sets out the recurring staff needs (end-to-end visibility, EPR-native workflow with no duplicate systems, confidence to act on a home result, less administrative burden), a draft end-to-end service journey, and an acute MVP framing in which HomeTest is the remote fulfilment layer while the clinical order, result review and onward care stay in the existing EPR. The PSA recommendations are to partner with a pathology supplier already able to run capillary PSA at scale and validate transdermal devices, partner with a digitally mature trust already delivering digital active surveillance, explore a panel-based approach combining PSA with other analytes, and build the service journey for both digital and non-digital patients. The checkpoint carries a two-site RDUH PSA pilot (day-one volume 50 to 100 per site) and an Epic-to-HomeTest-to-Epic proof of concept to prove the technical route before any live patient journey, with Salisbury and Southampton retained as EPR stress-test sites. It informs, and does not pre-empt, the SLT decisions on ChelWest partnership and adding a further site.
