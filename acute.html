    Dated record
    The urology session, 17 July

    The RDUH team, Professor Tim McDonald with urology nurses Paula and Kerry Rosewarne, demonstrated how PSA monitoring runs today inside Epic.

    Nurses pull a monthly report of PSA results, filter to their cohort and review each patient in turn, deciding whether action is needed, chasing investigations such as MRI, ordering the next PSA at typically three months, and routing patients through community collection.

    Result-triggered, not appointment-triggered

    The pathway moves on results rather than appointments. The PSA result is what keeps it going, so a delayed or lost result stalls the whole thing.

    That is the characteristic HomeTest has to design around, and it is why reliable result return matters more here than anywhere else in the programme.

    What they were positive and concerned about

    The team was positive off the back of their earlier PSA pilot, where patients liked the service and some were disappointed when it ended. They saw younger and working-age patients as the most likely adopters.

    Their primary concern was not patient acceptance but that results return reliably into Epic and existing clinical workflows.

    Transdermal device route

    OwnerOlivia Sharp / Simone Bilton with RDUH NextPre-pilot design Depends onImpress UKCA / MHRA timeline

    Impress is preferred: FDA-approved, awaiting UKCA, with MHRA timing unclear and possibly end of year. Tasso is the fallback. RDUH is checking Impress availability via Ronan and Nexus.

    Dated record
    Impress preferred, Tasso the fallback

    Impress is preferred: approved by the US Food and Drug Administration, awaiting UK Conformity Assessed (UKCA) marking, with the Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) timeline unclear and possibly end of year. RDUH is checking availability via Ronan O'Connor and Nexus.

    Tasso is the usable fallback: chunkier, with a slightly lower success rate, running on Royal Mail Track 24.

    The evidence capillary works for PSA

    RDUH's prior PSA pilot ran a post-radical-prostatectomy pathway on capillary sampling built on Epic: 917 invited, 310 sign-ups, 218 completed, and around 60 per cent wanting to continue.

    The learning was that one-to-one support for the first test and urology-team buy-in drive uptake, and that PSA itself lends well to capillary with a low failure rate.

    Why this is a scheduling dependency

    The 15 July senior leadership team (SLT) checkpoint puts the device decision ahead of the pilot as a pre-requisite, so regulatory timing on Impress feeds directly into whether the end-January 2027 day-one date holds.

    Tasso-versus-Impress user research sits in the RDUH workshop sequence for exactly this reason.

    PSA benefits / Best Practice Tariff case

    OwnerRachel Flower with Simon Leigh / Richard Samuel NextSubmission date TBC Depends onGIRFT data; factual vs estimated split

    Health economist Dr Simon Leigh is scoping the PSA active-surveillance benefits paper on the GIRFT pathway. Richard Samuel is sourcing GIRFT data and confirming the submission date.

    Dated record
    The argument

    Home testing helps trusts deliver the Getting It Right First Time (GIRFT) active-surveillance pathway more efficiently, releasing capacity, improving referral-to-treatment (RTT) performance and unlocking the associated incentive payments.

    Four-part structure

    First, the problem with today's outpatient-heavy, consultant-intensive pathway.

    Second, what changes when testing moves closer to the patient: which appointments, blood-taking visits, administrative touchpoints, missed appointments and delays disappear, and what becomes faster in risk identification, escalation and clinical decision-making.

    Third, the capacity released: consultant sessions freed, outpatient appointments avoided, additional first appointments and other referral-to-treatment drivers.

    Fourth, why the pathway cannot achieve the same benefits without patient-centric testing.

    The break-even point

    Simon Leigh is adding a break-even point so that benefit still accrues below the best case.

    That matters for how the case survives scrutiny: a benefits paper that only works at its most optimistic assumption tends not to.

    GIRFT data and the credibility risk

    Richard Samuel is checking whether GIRFT holds usable data and confirming the submission date.

    Separating factual from estimated figures is the live task and the main credibility risk on the paper.

    15 JulPSA Best Practice Tariff benefits paper scoped with the health economist

    Rachel Flower updated the team after a call with Dr Simon Leigh, the health economist, on the PSA Best Practice Tariff case and the numbers behind it. The paper will argue that home testing helps trusts deliver the GIRFT active-surveillance pathway more efficiently, releasing capacity, improving RTT performance and unlocking the associated incentive payments. It is structured in four parts: the problem with today's outpatient-heavy, consultant-intensive pathway; what changes when testing moves closer to the patient, meaning which appointments, blood-taking visits, administrative touchpoints, DNAs and delays disappear and what becomes faster in risk identification, escalation and clinical decision-making; the capacity released, such as consultant sessions freed, outpatient appointments avoided, additional first appointments and other RTT drivers; and why the pathway cannot achieve the same benefits without patient-centric testing. Simon will also work up a break-even point, showing financial benefit accruing even if only a limited number of patients take part, rather than only in a best case. Richard Samuel is finding out whether GIRFT holds usable data as a starting point and confirming the submission date, and a live question is how much of the capacity and change data is factual against assumed or estimated. A BPT working folder was set up under the Health Economist commercials area the same day. This is the acute pilot's benefits and investment case, distinct from the wider HomeTest tariff and unit-economics work.
