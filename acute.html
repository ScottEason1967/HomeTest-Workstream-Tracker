RDUH workshop sequence

OwnerOlivia Sharp / Annie Wakefield with RDUH NextPilot workshop Depends onRDUH urology team availability

The urology session has been held and written up (17 July). The sequence advances to the pilot workshop, then Tasso versus Impress user research, then a wider-tests workshop.

Full detail

The urology session on 17 July was the substantive one. The RDUH team, Professor Tim McDonald with urology nurses Paula and Kerry Rosewarne, demonstrated how PSA monitoring runs today inside Epic: nurses pull a monthly report of PSA results, filter to their cohort and review each patient in turn, deciding whether action is needed, chasing investigations such as MRI, ordering the next PSA at typically three months, and routing patients through community collection.

The important characteristic is that the pathway is result-triggered rather than appointment-triggered. The PSA result is what keeps it moving, so a delayed or lost result stalls the whole thing. That shapes what HomeTest has to guarantee.

The team was positive off the back of their earlier PSA pilot, where patients liked the service and some were disappointed when it ended, and saw younger and working-age patients as the most likely adopters. Their primary concern was not patient acceptance but that results return reliably into Epic and existing clinical workflows.

On the technical side, a first draft of the proof-of-concept architecture has been shared with RDUH. The next priority is a first draft of the Order API schemas for the acute-initiated pathway, covering both consumer and supplier sides, so the proof of concept can start. A pilot planning workshop with RDUH and Chelsea and Westminster is also named, to define the clinical study approach, timescales and the PSA proof of concept.

Beyond RDUH, the acute-initiated journey goes to the wider supplier market at Supplier Forum 3 on 22 July as a one-hour breakout led by Olivia Sharp, the first time the pattern has been tested outside bilateral conversations with RDUH.

Transdermal device route

OwnerOlivia Sharp / Simone Bilton with RDUH NextPre-pilot design Depends onImpress UKCA / MHRA timeline

Impress is preferred: FDA-approved, awaiting UKCA, with MHRA timing unclear and possibly end of year. Tasso is the fallback. RDUH is checking Impress availability via Ronan and Nexus.

Full detail

The pathway leans on a transdermal device. Impress is preferred: FDA-approved, awaiting UKCA, with the MHRA timeline unclear and possibly end of year. Tasso is the usable fallback, chunkier with a slightly lower success rate, running on Royal Mail Track 24. RDUH is checking Impress availability via Ronan O'Connor and Nexus.

The evidence that capillary works for this test comes from RDUH's prior PSA pilot, which ran a post-radical-prostatectomy pathway on capillary sampling built on Epic: 917 invited, 310 sign-ups, 218 completed, and around 60 per cent wanting to continue. The learning was that one-to-one support for the first test and urology-team buy-in drive uptake, and that PSA itself lends well to capillary with a low failure rate.

This is a scheduling dependency as much as a design one. The 15 July SLT checkpoint puts the device decision ahead of the pilot as a pre-requisite, so regulatory timing on Impress feeds directly into whether the end-January 2027 day-one date holds. Tasso-versus-Impress user research sits in the RDUH workshop sequence for exactly this reason.

PSA benefits / Best Practice Tariff case

OwnerRachel Flower with Simon Leigh / Richard Samuel NextSubmission date TBC Depends onGIRFT data; factual vs estimated split

Health economist Dr Simon Leigh is scoping the PSA active-surveillance benefits paper on the GIRFT pathway. Richard Samuel is sourcing GIRFT data and confirming the submission date.

Full detail

Rachel Flower scoped the paper with Dr Simon Leigh, the health economist. The argument is that home testing helps trusts deliver the GIRFT active-surveillance pathway more efficiently, releasing capacity, improving RTT performance and unlocking the associated incentive payments.

It is structured in four parts. First, the problem with today's outpatient-heavy, consultant-intensive pathway. Second, what changes when testing moves closer to the patient: which appointments, blood-taking visits, administrative touchpoints, DNAs and delays disappear, and what becomes faster in risk identification, escalation and clinical decision-making. Third, the capacity released, meaning consultant sessions freed, outpatient appointments avoided, additional first appointments and other RTT drivers. Fourth, why the pathway cannot achieve the same benefits without patient-centric testing.

Simon is adding a break-even point so that benefit still accrues below the best case, which matters for how the case survives scrutiny. Richard Samuel is checking whether GIRFT holds usable data and confirming the submission date. Separating factual from estimated figures is the live task and the main credibility risk.

29 JunRDUH site-visit notes filed: prior PSA pilot numbers and device direction

The prior RDUH PSA pilot ran a post-radical-prostatectomy pathway on capillary sampling built on Epic, with 917 invited, 310 sign-ups and 218 completed, around 60 per cent wanting to continue. The learning: one-to-one support for the first test and urology-team buy-in drive uptake, while PSA itself lends well to capillary with a low failure rate. Next steps sequenced as urology workshop, then pilot workshop, then Tasso-versus-Impress user research, then a wider-tests workshop. The pathway leans on a transdermal device, Impress preferred (FDA-approved, awaiting UKCA, MHRA timeline unclear and possibly end of year) with Tasso as fallback. Cerner/Epic integration complexity flagged for any RDUH-to-NW London (Chel West) multi-trust pilot.
